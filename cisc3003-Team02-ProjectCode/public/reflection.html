Chapter 07

What I learned building this

A personal closing chapter — less handbook, more journal. Lightly edited for readability; the thinking is ours.

We started this project thinking it would be a website about career advice. It turned out to be a website about the gap between "knowing" things about your career and actually doing anything about them. Most of our classmates already know they should study DSA, build projects and talk to professors. Knowing doesn't move the needle — writing things down, on a deadline, does.

What I set out to do

The original pitch was modest: build a responsive static site with the seven chapters required by the CISC3003 proposal. Nice typography, clean CSS, maybe a hamburger menu. Then the course requirements added signup, email verification, login, search, shortlist and history — and suddenly we were building a real product.

That changed everything about the project. The site stopped being a personal expression piece and became a tool that other CS students at UM could actually use. We started thinking about empty states, loading states, and what happens when a user's session expires mid-filter.

What changed along the way

Three decisions shifted under us.

From prose to data. In draft one, the Master's Preparation chapter was essay-form: long paragraphs about GRE preparation and timing. In draft two, we realised no student would read that wall of text. We pivoted to a timeline plus a searchable database of 37 programmes. The timeline is still prose-heavy, but now it's scannable — which matters when users are comparing five schools at once.

From one site to two layers. We originally wanted to avoid a backend. The course requirements forced our hand, and in retrospect we're glad. The backend is what makes the site a handbook you return to, not just one you read once. Saved programmes mean you can come back in three weeks and compare.

From "complete" to "good enough". We listed 60 Master's programmes in draft one and 120 job openings. After building the UI we cut that to 37 and 43. Quality of summary beats volume — a user reading ten honest one-sentence summaries makes better decisions than one skimming one hundred auto-generated ones.

Hardest design decisions

The hardest decision was the tier system for Master's programmes. Labelling schools T1/T2/T3 feels judgemental, and an honest system would include dozens of dimensions (research fit, advisor, funding, climate, cost, visa outcomes). We kept the tiers because the alternative — "here are 37 schools alphabetically" — is strictly less useful. We softened the blow by adding an explanatory paragraph and making the highlight sentence do more of the work than the tier label.

The second hardest decision was what to leave out. We considered adding PhD preparation, study-abroad exchanges, and internships-versus-research tradeoffs. Each would have been a reasonable chapter. We cut them because a well-executed seven-chapter handbook beats a half-finished ten-chapter one.

What I'd do differently

We'd start the backend earlier. We spent two weeks polishing typography on static HTML before hooking up the API, and when we finally did, several design assumptions had to be undone. Building the auth + shortlist + history loop end-to-end first, then layering the visual design on top, would have been faster overall.

We'd also write the Reflection chapter first, as a brief. Not to post it, but to force ourselves to articulate what we wanted readers to feel by the end. Writing the reflection last — as we're doing now — made us realise several content choices didn't serve the underlying thesis (that knowing and doing are different skills).

What this means for my own path

We each had a different takeaway. One of us is applying for Master's programmes next fall, and the timeline chapter is now the spreadsheet we're actually using. The other is leaning toward industry first, and the Jobs chapter became a shortlist of real applications, not just a display of curated openings. That's the right outcome — a handbook is only useful to the extent that it changes what you do on Monday morning.

If you're reading this and found even one programme, one career path, or one sentence that made you write something down today, we'll consider the project a success. The version of us that existed six weeks ago, when we first scoped this site, didn't know what we were building. The version of us writing this knows — and that is the actual deliverable.

I didn't build this because I figured it out. I built it because writing it down was how I figured it out. — Group 02

Thanks for reading

If you found this useful, the best next step is one of three things: sign up and save a programme you'll actually apply to, open Skills and pick one to develop this month, or go back to the Home page and start from the top with fresh eyes.
