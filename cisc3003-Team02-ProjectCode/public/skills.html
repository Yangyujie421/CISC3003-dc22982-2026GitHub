Chapter 02

Skills development

A concrete catalogue of what to build and when — across technical depth, soft skills, and the meta-skill of learning.

A typical undergraduate mastery curve — yours will differ, but this is roughly what employers and graduate committees see.

Programming fundamentals

Typically Year 1

Fluency in one general-purpose language (Java / Python / C++) plus the muscle memory to refactor without fear.

Soft skills are the reason two technically-equivalent candidates get different offers. They're learnable, but they require deliberate practice — not osmosis.

  1. 1

    Communication

    Explain a technical decision to a non-technical listener in under two minutes. The clarity forces clarity in your own thinking.

  2. 2

    Collaboration

    Ship one meaningful feature in a shared codebase with at least one other human. PR reviews, disagreements and merge conflicts included.

  3. 3

    Written clarity

    Learn to write a one-paragraph project summary that makes a reader actually understand what the project does.

  4. 4

    Time management

    Block time on a calendar the way you block time for exams. Weekly review on Sunday. This is boring and works.

  5. 5

    Asking good questions

    Before asking, write the question as if it were a forum post: context, what you tried, what you expected. Half the time you answer it yourself.

  6. 6

    Receiving feedback

    Separate "this criticism is wrong" from "this criticism feels bad". Both can be true; only one matters for the work.

Meta-skills compound. A small investment early in the semester pays back every single week afterwards.

How to learn fast

Active retrieval beats re-reading. After reading a chapter, close the book and write what you remember. The gap between what you thought you knew and what you can produce is the actual knowledge.

How to read documentation

First pass: read only the headings. Second pass: read only the first paragraph of each section. Third pass: read the full section that's actually relevant to your task. You almost never need to read the whole thing.

How to use AI tools responsibly

Use AI to shorten the "lookup loop", not to skip the understanding loop. If you can't explain the code the assistant gave you, don't commit it. Your interviewer will notice.
