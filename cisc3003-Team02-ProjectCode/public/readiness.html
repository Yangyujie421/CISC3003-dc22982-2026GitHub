Chapter 06

Job readiness

Resume → Portfolio → Apply → Interview. A four-stage workflow that gets most CS students from "interested" to "offered".

Four stages

1

Resume

One focused page, technical roles only.

2

Portfolio

Two to four projects that demonstrate depth.

3

Apply

A tracked, deadline-aware application workflow.

4

Interview

Behavioural, technical and — for some roles — system design.

Stage 1

Resume

Structure

One page. Sections in order: Contact, Education, Experience, Projects, Skills. Put Skills last — interviewers use it as a keyword match, not a conversation starter.

Content rules

Every bullet starts with a verb and ends with a metric. "Built", "Shipped", "Automated", "Reduced". Numbers beat adjectives. "Reduced model training time by 34%" beats "significantly improved performance".

Common mistakes

Listing every class you've ever taken. Listing a skill without the context in which you used it. Two pages. Including a photo. Long paragraphs instead of bullets. Decorative fonts. Any of these can get you filtered out before a human reads it.

Stage 2

Portfolio

A portfolio is not a long list. It's 2–4 projects with depth. For each:

  • A one-line "what it is" — understandable in ten seconds.
  • Three bullets on technical decisions you made and why.
  • A working demo (not just a GitHub link). Hosted, with a clear README.
  • A screenshot or short video so a reviewer can decide in seconds whether to click through.

The best entry-level portfolios show a range: one full-stack web app, one systems or performance project, one ML or data project.

Project A

Full-stack web

Real users + non-trivial database + deployed.

Project B

Systems or perf

A parser, a small VM, or a perf-tuned tool.

Project C

ML / data

An end-to-end pipeline, not just a notebook.

Project D

Optional flourish

Open source contributions count here.

Stage 3

Apply

  • Open a spreadsheet the day you start. Columns: company, role, deadline, status, referral contact, follow-up date.
  • New-grad applications for summer 20XX starts typically open in July/August of 20(XX-1). Apply early — companies fill quotas before deadlines.
  • Cold outreach works more often than you'd expect, especially to recruiters on LinkedIn. Be specific: mention the role, why you fit, and the link to your resume.
  • Ask for a referral from every classmate and alumnus who already works at your target companies.
  • Expect a 5–10% response rate from cold applications. A 60%+ response rate from warm referrals. The math is obvious.

Stage 4

Interview

Use the STAR framework (Situation, Task, Action, Result). Prepare 4–5 stories covering conflict, ownership, failure, teamwork, ambiguity. One story can often be reused across questions with a different framing.

  • Tell me about yourself
  • A time you disagreed
  • A time you failed

Prepare 150 LeetCode problems across arrays, strings, trees, graphs, DP, heaps, and hashmaps. Quality of explanation matters as much as correctness. Practise talking out loud while coding.

  • Two Sum variants
  • Tree traversals
  • Graph BFS/DFS
  • Sliding window

Not always asked at entry-level but increasingly common. Read Designing Data-Intensive Applications chapters 1–9. Practise designing: URL shortener, chat app, news feed. Focus on asking clarifying questions before drawing boxes.

  • URL shortener
  • Rate limiter
  • News feed

Internship vs. full-time

Internship

Lower bar, shorter commitment, high return-offer rate. If you're Year 2–3, applying for internships is almost always the right move. Summer offers converted to full-time beat the fall recruiting pipeline.

Full-time

Higher bar, longer commitment, more thorough interview loop. If you're Year 4 and already have intern experience, this is the path. If you don't have intern experience, consider a gap year to intern first.
